Why hashdiff relies on this
01
Deterministic. Same input always gives the same hash — so comparing two loads of the same unchanged record always agrees.
02
Sensitive to tiny changes. Flip one character anywhere in the row and the hash looks completely different — nothing is "almost the same."
03
Fixed size, no matter the input. A satellite row with 3 columns or 30 columns still compresses down to one hash of the same length — cheap to store and compare.
04
One comparison instead of many. This is the whole payoff: instead of checking FILE_DATE, STATUS, and AMOUNT separately, you check one value and trust it reflects all of them.
Anchor phrase: "A hash takes anything, of any length, and turns it into a fixed-size fingerprint — and even a one-character change produces a totally different fingerprint. That's what makes it safe to trust a single hashdiff comparison instead of checking every column."
Deep dive — inside the blender

Inside SHA-256 / what the function actually does

This is the real algorithm running in your browser — not a metaphor. Type something, then scrub through the 64 mixing rounds and watch eight registers churn your input into a fingerprint.

1 · Input becomes blocks
Your text → bytes → padded to a 512-bit block. Padding includes the message length, so "abc" and "abc " are already different before mixing even starts.
2 · The message schedule feeds the rounds
The block is expanded into 64 words — the first 16 are your input, the next 48 are made by rotating and XORing earlier words. Every input bit gets fed into the machine again and again.
3 · 64 rounds of stirring — scrub through them
Eight registers (a–h). Each round: rotate, XOR, add — then every register shifts one seat down and the new mix lands in a. Drag the slider and watch bits smear.

Registers start from fixed constants (fractional parts of √2, √3, √5… — "nothing up my sleeve" numbers).
4 · The avalanche, bit by bit
Flip one character of the input and compare digests. Lit cells are bits that flipped. A good hash flips ~50% — watch it happen at every round, not just the end.

This is why your hashdiff catches a trailing space, a case change, a reordered column — any change avalanches. One equality check replaces twenty column comparisons.
The four properties, now visible
Deterministic — same input, same churn, same digest, every time.
Fixed length — 8 registers × 32 bits = 256 bits out, whether you hash "a" or a whole satellite row.
One-way — after 64 rounds of rotate/XOR/add, un-stirring is computationally hopeless.
Avalanche — one bit in, half the bits out. The property your entire vault leans on.
